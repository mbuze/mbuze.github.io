Research

Outline of current research interests (to be regularly updated)

My current main research interest concerns developing a mathematically rigorous theory of fracture at an atomistic scale. The term fracture refers to a separation of a material into two or more pieces by means of a thin opening, a crack, propagating through it due to the accumulation of stress. Cracks are abundant in human-built structures and are, for instance, a common sight in the walls of ageing buildings, as seen to the right in the a photo of a crack in the wall of my student accomodation at Warwick.

The atomistic scale is known to lie at the heart of fracture, with cracks propagating via atomistic mechanisms involving breaking interaction bonds between individual atoms. If one zooms in on a crack tip, firstly it leads to a simplified geometry and then the atomistic structure is revealed, as illustrated below.

The following is a gist of my research interests, a more detailed treatment is to be found in the publications themselves, with freely available preprint available in the Publications section of this webpage.

Atomistic brittle fracture

In brittle fracture, with no plastic deformation occurring around the crack tip, the fundamental insight of the standard continuum fracture mechanics lies in identifying a single parameter, known as *the stress intensity factor (SIF)* and showing that if it exceeds a material-dependent critical value, then it is energetically favourable for the crack to propagate.

The key phenomenon in atomistic fracture is lattice trapping, a term referring to there existing a range of values for the stress intensity factor for which the crack remains locally stable, i.e. lattice-trapped, thus conceptually invalidating the key insight of the continuum theory. The critical SIF computed directly from finite-size atomistic models lies somewhere in-between and is known to deviate from the continuum theory prediction.

My work in this regards is at the junction of the mathematical analysis of the underlying modelling, combined with proposing novel insights leading to quantifiable improvements in the accuracy of the corresponding computer simulations. Here are some snippets for illustration.

Atomistic near-crack-tip plasticity

In ductile fracture, the stresses near the crack tip lead to a build up topological defects known as dislocation, carriers of plastic deformation, which can potential shield the material from further crack propagation.

In a recent independent endeavour, I have introduced a sophisticated geometric and functional framework of the lattice manifold complex, in which a cracked crystalline material is described by a Riemann surface corresponding to the complex square root mapping. The ongoing work is to capitalise on this to address several issues related to the mathematics of near-crack-tip plasticity, such as considering the rigorous passage from the atomistic scale to the mesoscopic scale.
